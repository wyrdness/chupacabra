## Overview

The Chupacabra ('goat-sucker') is among the youngest internationally recognised cryptids. The phenomenon erupted in Puerto Rico in 1995, when a wave of livestock killings — primarily goats, sheep, and chickens, found with puncture wounds and reportedly drained of blood — was attributed to an unknown creature. Witness Madelyne Tolentino's August 1995 description, given to journalists in Canóvanas, fixed the popular image of an upright, bipedal, spined creature with large red eyes. From the late 1990s the Chupacabra label spread across Latin America and into the United States, where reports increasingly described a different animal: a hairless, dog-like quadruped. Necropsies of several alleged southwestern-US Chupacabra carcasses, including the 2007 Cuero, Texas specimen recovered by Phylis Canion, identified them as canids — coyotes, coyote-dog hybrids, or grey foxes — affected by Sarcoptes scabiei (sarcoptic mange). Folklorist and investigator Benjamin Radford spent five years researching the phenomenon and concluded in his 2011 book Tracking the Chupacabra that Tolentino's foundational 1995 description was substantively shaped by the alien creature 'Sil' from the science-fiction film Species (1995), which she had seen shortly before her sighting. The Chupacabra is now treated by mainstream folklorists as a 20th-century legend whose physical-evidence cases reduce to mange-affected canids and other known animals.

## Notable Sightings

### Madelyne Tolentino sighting ⭐
> **Date**: 1995-08-01  
> **Location**: Canóvanas, eastern Puerto Rico  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Local resident (Madelyne Tolentino))  
> **Evidence**: Press interviews (1995)  
Tolentino described a bipedal creature standing roughly 1.2 m tall, with large oval black eyes, a row of spines along its back, three-toed feet, and powerful hind legs. The description was published in Puerto Rican press and is the foundational image of the Chupacabra.
**Credibility**: low — The most-cited single Chupacabra report; the basis for Radford's debunking work.
**Sources**: El Nuevo Día (1995); Radford, B. (2011), Tracking the Chupacabra

### Elmendorf Beast ⭐
> **Date**: 2004-01-01  
> **Location**: Elmendorf, Bexar County, Texas  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Rancher (Devin McAnally))  
> **Evidence**: Body specimen; Texas Parks and Wildlife identification  
Devin McAnally shot a hairless canid that had been killing chickens on his property near Elmendorf, Texas. The animal's appearance — hairless, blueish skin, large fangs — was widely reported as a Chupacabra. The Texas Parks and Wildlife Department identified it as a coyote affected by sarcoptic mange.
**Credibility**: medium — Foundational case for the 'mange-affected canid' explanation of US Chupacabra reports.
**Sources**: Texas Parks and Wildlife identification (2004); Associated Press coverage (2004)

### Cuero, Texas carcass (Phylis Canion) ⭐
> **Date**: 2007-07-01  
> **Location**: Outside Cuero, DeWitt County, Texas  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Rancher (Phylis Canion))  
> **Evidence**: Body specimen; DNA sequencing work (2010)  
Phylis Canion recovered the carcass of a hairless, fanged canid outside Cuero after a series of chicken killings. The body was preserved and later sequenced by researchers at Texas State University and the University of California, Davis, who identified it as a coyote with Mexican wolf ancestry.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Extraterrestrial origin** _(Jorge Martín, 1990s Puerto Rican press)_

Hypothesis, popular in mid-1990s reporting, that the Chupacabra was an alien creature, possibly escaped from a covert US research site in Puerto Rico (often associated with the El Yunque rainforest).

### Scientific

**Undescribed Caribbean predator** _(1990s-era cryptozoologists)_

Early hypothesis (largely abandoned) that the 1995 wave was caused by an unknown predator endemic to Puerto Rico.

### Skeptical

**Mange-affected canids (Texan/Mexican type)** _(Barry O'Connor (University of Michigan), Texas Parks and Wildlife, Texas State University / UC Davis researchers)_

The hairless 'Chupacabra' carcasses recovered in Texas, Mexico, and elsewhere are coyotes, coyote-dog hybrids, or grey foxes severely affected by Sarcoptes scabiei (sarcoptic mange), which produces hair loss, thickened skin, and emaciation.

**Cinematic source for the Puerto Rican type** _(Benjamin Radford)_

Tolentino's 1995 foundational description is substantively shaped by the alien creature 'Sil' from the science-fiction film Species (1995), which she had seen shortly before her sighting.

**Feral dogs and known predators** _(Puerto Rican Department of Agriculture veterinarians)_

The 1995 livestock-killing wave in Puerto Rico is attributable to feral dogs, mongooses, and other known predators, with deaths consistent with predator throat punctures rather than exsanguination.
